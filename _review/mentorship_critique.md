# Mentorship Page Review

## Intro Critique

### Current text

> I mentored 10+ students during my Ph.D., from summer scholars to Ph.D. students, on time series foundation models, clinical AI, and evaluation. Most collaborations led to publications at ICML, ICLR, NeurIPS, or AAAI. Mentees have gone on to Ph.D. programs at CMU, Cornell, and Georgia Tech, research positions at ETH Zurich, and industry roles.

### What's wrong

1. **Reads like a resume bullet, not a mentorship statement.** It lists credentials and outcomes but says nothing about what mentorship actually means to you or how you approach it. A prospective student reading this learns that you have metrics, not that you care about working with them.

2. **"10+ students" and venue-dropping feels performative.** Leading with a count and a list of top venues signals "look how productive I am," not "I want to help you grow." The venues already appear on your publications page.

3. **No signal that you're open to new mentees.** A student visiting this page to figure out whether they can work with you gets no invitation, no sense of what you look for, and no indication of whether you're currently taking students.

4. **Missing: your actual philosophy.** What do you value in a mentoring relationship? Do you care about giving students ownership of problems? Do you emphasize technical depth, taste, or independence? The current text offers nothing a visitor couldn't scrape from your CV.

### Rewrite options

**Option A -- Direct and concise:**

> I work closely with students at all levels -- undergraduates, master's, and doctoral students -- on problems in time series foundation models, clinical AI, and evaluation. I care about giving mentees real ownership of research questions and helping them build the judgment to tackle open problems independently. If you're interested in working together, reach out.

**Option B -- Slightly more personal, references outcomes without leading with them:**

> Some of the most rewarding work I've done has been with students. I've mentored undergraduates, summer scholars, and Ph.D. students on time series foundation models, clinical AI, and evaluation -- several of these collaborations became papers at ICML, NeurIPS, and ICLR, and mentees have gone on to Ph.D. programs and research positions. I try to give students genuine ownership of hard problems from the start. I'm always open to hearing from students who want to collaborate.

**Option C -- Shortest, most direct:**

> I mentor students on time series foundation models, clinical AI, and evaluation. I prioritize giving mentees ownership of real research problems early. If you're a student interested in collaborating, please get in touch.

**Recommendation:** Option A or C. Option B is acceptable but starts drifting toward the self-congratulatory tone of the original. Whichever you pick, the key improvements are: (1) say what you actually value in mentorship, (2) explicitly invite prospective students to reach out, (3) stop leading with counts and venues.
